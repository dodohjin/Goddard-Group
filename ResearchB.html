B2
Heterogeneous Catalysis
Learn More

The developments in understanding homogenous catalysis rely heavily on experimental validation through NMR and crystallography. In contrast, there is little in the way of precise methods for establishing the reaction mechanisms of heterogeneous catalysts. Even so, Emily Carter using very oversimplified QM in the earlier 1980’s explained Ag catalyzed epoxidation (funded by Shell), showing that the selectivity was not limited to 5/6. major focus in the 1980’s and. 1990’s was on ammoxidation of propene to acrylic acid and acrylonitrile, with modest funding from SOHIO (with Bob Grasselli). Despite the limitations of early QM, we were able to develop concepts such as spectator oxo and spectator imido-induced activation, that have been validated with modern DFT calculations. More recently, Mu-Jeng Cheng and I were able to explain the mechanism underlying the VPO catalyzed butane to maleic acid (14 reaction steps) which led to a new principle for CH activation: reduction-coupled oxo activation (ROA) by which P=O or Te=O oxo bonds can activate the strong CH bonds of alkanes. This concept also explains the propane activation by the M1 phase of the MoVNbTeOx ammoxidation catalysts, and is, I believe, the mechanism for a great many selective oxidation metal oxide catalysts.

B3
Electrocatalysis
Learn More

In recent years, our interest in renewable energy (and funding from DOE JCAP, DOE LiSA, and NSF CBET) has led us to develop new QM methods for doing full solvent metadynamics and Grand Canonical QM (constant potential) with applications to fuel cells, water splitting (to produce H2 and. O2), and CO2 reduction to theane and ethanl. Amazingly, these full solvent QM-metaMD and GC-QM calculations lead to accuracies in the free energy activation energies of 0.05 eV and onset potentials to 0.05 V for the cases we have examined. From these full solvent QM, we are now extracting XPS shifts and vibrational frequencies for the surface intermediates to serve as targets as experimental methods are developed to probe the electrode–electrolyte interface (EEI). Since these QM studies are limited to 200 atoms for 20 ps, we have used the ReaxFF to equilibrate the full solvent water for a nanosecond to then do QM MD for 10–20 ps. In addition to characterize the active sites responsible for the dramatically improved performance of 10–20 nanoparticles (200,000 atoms), we have used ReaxFF to simulate CVD growth and then use ReaxFF to identify the active sites, the best of which we can apply QM to determine the surface structures responsible. A recent development is the RexPoN embedded QM (ReQM) in which the full 1000 to million atom system is described with the new RexPoN force field that matches the long-range nonbond and electrostatic interactions of QM so that we can embed the QM in just the 200 atom region involved in the reaction, while retaining the proper long-range interactions. This is called RexPoN embedded QM or ReQM.

B3-1. CO2 reduction to ethylene, ethanol, and other hydrocarbon & alcohol products
  • #1466. “ CO2 reduction on pure Cu produces only H2 after subsurface O is depleted: Theory and experiment”.

    This paper predicted the rates of ethylene, Methane, and H2 for Cu(111) surface and for Cu2O surface showing comparable rates for ethylene and H2 for Cu2O but ~ 1000 times more H2 than ethylene for Cu(111). Experiments carried out on Cu(111) and Cu2O showed initial production of all 3, but that after 25 hours both systems led to ~ 1000 times more H2 than ethylene, but re-exposing the surface to air, led again to ethylene production. This shows that the ethylene production requires surface O that is depleted during reduction.

  • #1523. "Structural, dynamic, and diffusion properties of a Li6(PS4)SCl superionic conductor from molecular dynamics simulations; prediction of a dramatically improved conductor".

    Our new understanding of the detailed atomistic mechanisms underlying the fast Li-ion diffusion in Li6(PS4)SCl provides guidelines to help design advanced solid state electrolytes with improved characteristics suitable for application in a new generation of Li batteries. Thus we predicted that Li5(PS4) Cl2 with 7 vacancies per 5 Li would increase Li diffusivity whereas Li7(PS4)S2 with 5 vacancies per 7 Li would decrease the corresponding Li-ion conductivity. To verify this prediction we carried out 20 ns MD simulations on Li5(PS4)Cl2 leading to a Li-ion conductivity of 64.8 mS/cm, 10 times higher than that of Li6(PS4)SCl (7.25 mS/cm) and on Li7(PS4)S2 (0.756 mS/cm), leading to a Li-ion conductivity 10 times lower than that of Li6(PS4)SCl.

B10
Amorphous Metals
Learn More

The G and L phases of amorphous metals:

In a series of papers we have established that there are two quire different amorphous phases for metals.

  • The L Phase which is a homogeneous supercooled liquid leading to very low shear moduli
  • The G Phased which is amorphous but exhibits shear rigidity due to a heterogenous structure consisting of solid-like cores (dark blue) embedded into a L-phase like matrix (light blue)

Figure 1366. Enthalpic relation of the L-phase, G-phase, and crystal.

The undercooled liquid undergoes a first order configurational freezing transition from the high temperature disordered liquid-phase (L) to a metastable, heterogeneous, configurationally ordered phase that displays elastic rigidity with a persistent and finite shear modulus. The characteristics of this ordered phase lead us to designate it as G-phase, which we consider to a glass. We show that the L-G transition occurs by nucleation of the G-phase from the L-phase. Both the L- and G-phases are metastable phases since both ultimately crystallize at these temperatures. The observed first order transition is reversible: the G-phase displays a first order melting transition to the L-phase at a coexistence temperature, TG,M.


Figure 1502. L-Glass and G-glass formation in the binary Cu2Zr system. (C) Atomic structure of the L-phase for Cu2Zr composition at 300 K. (D) Atomic structures of the G-phase for Cu2Zr at 300 K. The icosahedral and liquid atoms determined from the Honeycutt-Anderson analysis are represented by purple and yellow balls, respectively.
