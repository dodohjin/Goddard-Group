A1-1. Grand Canonical Potential Kinetics (GCP-K) or Grand Canonical QM (GC-QM)

This provides a practical approach to maintain the reactant state, the transition state and the product state at the same applied potential for all reaction steps. This contrasts with standard QM which keep the number of electrons fixed for he reactant state, the transition state and the product state.

  • #1254. “Grand canonical electronic density-functional theory: Algorithms and applications to electrochemistry”.

    Extends normal QM with fixed numbers of electrons to constant external potential, where the number of electrons can change as the reaction proceeds. For use in electrocatalysis.

  • #1292. “The reaction mechanism for the hydrogen evolution reaction on the basal plane sulfur vacancy site of MoS2 using grand canonical potential kinetics”.

    The first paper presenting the GCP-K method.


    • Figure 1292. (a) QM predicted I−V curves for acidic and basic conditions. (b) Tafel plots for acidic and basic conditions. (c) Comparison between our QM predictions and the experimental interpolation. This shows excellent agreement.

  • #1405. “Oxygen evolution reaction over catalytic single-site Co in a well-defined brookite TiO₂ nanorod surface”.

    Applied GCP-K to Co/TiO2 single crystal nanoparticles. Excellent results. If experiment is exact then QM barrier is 0.03 eV too low!


    • Figure 1405. Direct comparison of the experimental results and GC-QM predictions for Co-TiO2. (a) Tafel slope comparison of the experimental result with AEM and LOM predictions. The experimental current density of Co-TiO2 is normalized over the ECSA. (b) Turn-Over Frequencies (TOF) from experiment and GCQM predictions. The predicted Tafel slopes and TOFs are in excellent agreement with the experimental values. The error bars represent the standard deviation of the experimental TOFs determined from five independent samples.

  • #1496. “Reaction Mechanism and Strategy for Optimizing the Hydrogen Evolution Reaction on Single-Layer 1T′ WSe2 and WTe2 Based on Grand Canonical Potential Kinetics”.

    This considers a single anion vacancy and a single periodic layer. The anion vacancy can bind 0 to 3 H atoms leading to 3 Volmer steps, 3 Heyrovsky steps, and 2 Tafel reactions. The interplay between the rates leads to a J that is not monotonic in U. The predicted onset potential (at 10 mA/cm−2) is −0.53 V for WSe2 (experiment is −0.51 V) and −0.51 V for WTe2 (experiment is −0.57 V).

(e,f) Plots of reaction cycles as a function of the applied potential for (e) WSe2 and (f) WTe2.


A1-2. GVB methods for QM

Starting with his PhD thesis in October 1964 and then with his appointment as a Noyes Research Fellow in November 1964, Goddard helped revolutionize the methods of QM. These first methods combining spin symmetry with the Pauli Principle are often known as Generalized Valence Bond, the first methods to determine the shape of orbitals for correlated wavefunctions. Key papers include:

  • #53. "Generalized Valence Bond Description of Bonding in Low-Lying States of Molecules".

    This showed how GVB enables new conceptual models for understanding the states of molecules.

  • #83. "Orbital optimization in electronic wave functions; equations for quadratic and cubic convergence of general multiconfiguration wave functions".

    This completed the developments by Goddard from 1970 to 1976 for optimization of complex open shell wavefunctions such as GVB, MC-SCR, and CAS-SCF.


A1-3. New developments in DFT

Starting with his PhD thesis in October 1964 and then with his appointment as a Noyes Research Fellow in November 1964, Goddard helped revolutionize the methods of QM. These first methods combining spin symmetry with the Pauli Principle are often known as Generalized Valence Bond, the first methods to determine the shape of orbitals for correlated wavefunctions. Key papers include:

  • #593. "Accurate Energies and Structures for Large Water Clusters Using the X3LYP Hybrid Density Functional".

    We predict structures and energies of water clusters containing up to 19 waters with X3LYP, an extended hybrid density functional designed to describe noncovalently bound systems as accurately as covalent systems. Our work establishes X3LYP as the most practical ab initio method today for calculating accurate water cluster structures and energies. We compare X3LYP/aug-cc-pVTZ energies to the most accurate theoretical values available (n = 2-6, 8), MP2 with basis set superposition error (BSSE) corrections extrapolated to the complete basis set limit. Our energies match these reference energies remarkably well, with RMS difference of 0.1 kcal/mol/water. X3LYP also has ten times less BSSE than MP2 with similar basis sets, allowing one to neglect BSSE at moderate basis sizes. The net result is that X3LYP is  100 times faster than canonical MP2 for moderately sized water clusters.

  • #952. "A fast doubly hybrid density functional method close to chemical accuracy using a local opposite spin ansatz".

    Culmination of a series of papers developing improved methods for DFT. Includes double excitations to describe dispersion interactions within the DFT with no empirical corrections, XYGJ-OS leads to reaction barriers within 1.1 kcal/mol and to van der Waals complexes with energies accurate to 0.5 kcal/mol, yet it scales as N**3.


A1-4. Extending practical ab initio QM throughout the periodic table using Effective Core Potentials

The Goddard group developed and validated the theory for rigorously replacing the core electrons of large atoms using angular momentum projected core effective potentials based on ab initio pseudo-orbitals modified to retain norm conservation. These developments from 1969-1977 became the basis for the norm-conserving pseudopotentials ubiquitous in modern quantum chemical and solid state physics calculations.

  • #9. "New foundation for the use of pseudopotentials in metals".

    The fundamental idea is to get an ab initio rigorous pseudopotential by directly inverting the ab initio orbital after eliminating nodes.

  • #63. "Ab initio effective potentials for use in molecular quantum mechanics".

    Showed how to obtain an accurate ab initio pseudopotential (the effective core potential, ECP) from valence HF orbitals by mixing core orbitals so that the modified HF pseudo-orbital would go smoothly to zero at the nucleus but using a separate radial form for each angular momentum projection to account properly for the effect of the missing core electrons.

  • #62. "Fe and Ni AB initio effective potentials for use in molecular calculations".

    Validated the ECP concept by deriving the ECP for Fe and Ni and showing that it accurately reproduced states of various ionization of Fe and Ni and of FeH. This approach was then applied to the rest of the periodic table by former Goddard group students Hay and Wadt to obtain the ECP (pseudo-potentials) that served as the basis for practical simulations on the whole periodic table. The method was popularized by Hamann, Schlüter, and Chiang in 1979, who called it "Norm-Conserving Pseudopotentials".

ReaxFF and RexPoN extend the ability of describing chemical reactions of systems from the QM level: < 300 atoms (2 nm) less than 100 ps to the range of scales of 10 nm (100,000 atoms) to 30 nm (1 million atoms) with time scales up to 20 ns.

A2-2. RexPoN

RexPoN describes the noncovalent interactions using

  • nonbond two body van der Waals interactions deriveved from PBE-D3 QM calculations of the Equations of State for H2, graphite, N2, O2, F2, Ne etc.
  • Electrostatics from the PQEq model in with the charge transfer is described by electronefavity derived from atomic ionization potential and electron affinity with the chare described by a Gaussian of the size of the atom. The Gaussian electron shell is allowed to move off the core in response to the net electric field, but restaine4d by a harmonic potential.
  • Hydrogen bond interactions depend on both the angle of the OH bod of the donor and the lone pair direction of the acceptor.

A3
Generic Nonreactive Force Fields (UFF, Dreiding)
Learn More

Force fields (FF) are usually developed to describe specific classes of systems. Thus CHARMM and Amber for proteins, nucleic acids, and carbohydrates are restricted mostly to H, C, N, O, P, S with many special parameters for various bond types. Similarly for OPLS which describes proteins and organic polymers.

But for materials we want to describe the whole periodic table. Thus we need generic force fields that cover all combinations of atoms, but possibly less accurate for particular cases than specialized FF. We have developed two such generic FF.

The universal FF (UFF) has parameters for the whole periodic table up to Lr (Z=103) but just 6 parameter per atom. The DREIDING Force field covers the nonmetallic part of the periodic table: C, N, O, F columns.

A3-1. UFF
  • #275. “UFF, a full periodic table force field for molecular mechanics and molecular dynamics simulations”.
    The force field parameters are described using general rules based only on the element, its hybridization, and its connectivity. There are 6 parameters per atom.
    Bond Radius: Ri. The Bond distance between atoms j and k is Rjk = Rj + Rk + RBOjk + RENjk
    Where RBOjk = - 0.1332 (Rj + Rk ) ln (BOjk) where BO is the bond order.
    The electronegative correction is RENjk = Rj*Rk [Sqrt(cj)- Sqrt(ck)]2/ (Rj*cj + Rk*k)
    where cj is the electronegativity from the QEq method of predicting charges.
    Atomic force constant: Zj. The two body force constant is Kjk = 642.12*Zj*Zk/Rjk**3
    The angle bending force constant Kijk is also based on Rj and Kj
    The nonbond term is taken as exponential 6 with three parameters: R0, D0, and ζ. But in LAMMPS the default is LJ12-6 with the same R0 and D0, Here ρ = R/R0

    $$\begin{gathered} E^{\mathrm{LJ}}=D_0\left[\rho^{-12}-2 \rho^{-6}\right] \end{gathered}$$ $$\begin{gathered}E^{\mathrm{X6}}=D_0\left[\left(\frac{6}{\zeta-6}\right) e^{\Im(1-\rho)}-\left(\frac{\zeta}{\zeta-6}\right) \rho^{-6}\right] \end{gathered}$$

  • #264. "Charge equilibration for molecular dynamics simulations".

    Developed general method for predicting charges using atomic averaged ionization potential (IP) and electron affinity (EA) to define


    Electronegativity: $$\chi_i^0=1 / 2(I P+E A)=\left(\frac{\partial E}{\partial q}\right)_{i 0},$$
    and Hardness: $$\begin{gathered}J_{i i}^0=I P-E A=\left(\frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial q^2}\right)_{i 0} \end{gathered}$$
    So that the atomic electrostatic energy becomes $$\left\{E_i^0+\chi_i^0 q_i+\frac{1}{2} J_{i i}^0 q_i^2+\frac{1}{2} K_s r_{i c, i s}^2\right\}$$where q=0 for the neutral atom.
    Then the total electrostatic energy becomes$$\begin{aligned} E\left(\left\{\vec{r}_{i c}, \vec{r}_{i s}, q_i\right\}\right)= & \sum_i^N\left\{E_i^0+\chi_i^0 q_i+\frac{1}{2} J_{i i}^0 q_i^2+\frac{1}{2} K_s r_{i c, i s}^2\right\} \\ & +\sum_{i k>j l} C_{i k, j l}\left(\vec{r}_{i k, j l}\right) q_{i k} q_{j l} \end{aligned}$$

    Here the interaction with charges on other centers is not qi*qj/Rij because each charge is delocalized over the atom. Initially we used Slater type exponential functions, but later we switched to Gaussian functions for the ease of taking derivatives. Thus the C includes this shielding.
    The chemical potential at each atom is μi = dEi/dqi which we require be the same on all atoms. This leads to N-1 equations. The N equation is that the sum of all charges adds up to the number of electrons in the system, usually 0.
