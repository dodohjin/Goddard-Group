  • #1512. "Electron-catalysed molecular recognition".

    Design of an electron-catalysed molecular recognition process.

    (a) Structural formulae and graphical representations of the host–guest system, which includes a macrocyclic host, R2(•+) and a dumbbell-shaped guest, D+(•+).

    Electron catalysis arises from the fact that each BIPY unit in the system has three redox states, dicationic (blue), radical cationic (purple) and neutral (red)

    (b) Mechanism for an electron-catalysed molecular recognition process. The direct (grey dashed arrow) formation of a trisradical complex [D⊂R]+3(•+) from R2(•+) and D+(•+) is all but kinetically forbidden. The catalytic (black solid arrows) complexation comprises four steps,

    • Step 1: injection of an electron.
    • Step 2: a single-electron reduction of either one of the BIPY•+ units in R2(•+) or the BIPY•+ unit in D+(•+).
    • Step 3: the rapid formation of a bisradical complex [D⊂R]+2(•+), the key intermediate, favoured by a decrease in the Coulombic repulsion between the host and guest molecules.
    • Step 4: oxidation of the bisradical complex [D⊂R]+2(•+), producing a trisradical complex [D⊂R]+3(•+) as the final product, while releasing an electron to close the catalytic cycle.

    The electron is formally written into the catalytic cycle to highlight its role as a catalyst, this does not imply the existence of a free electron during the catalytic process.

  • #1553. "An electric molecular motor".



    Working mechanism of the [3]catenane molecular motor [3]CMM.

    (a) Optimized quantum mechanical model structures with superimposed space-filling representations) for the [3]catenane [3]CMM, including the oxidized [3]CMM13+ (Top) and reduced [3]CMM7+6• (Bottom) states.

    (b) A 2D-position map describing the movement of two CBPQT4+/2(+•) rings around the loop. The x and y axes represent the positions of the CBPQT4+/2(+•) rings on the loop. The trajectories indicate that, for unidirectional motion, there are a Coulombic barrier (PY+) and a steric barrier (IPP) under reducing and oxidizing conditions, respectively. The dashed red diagonal line represents a barrier that cannot be crossed physically.

    (c) The redox operation of [3]CMM13+/7+6• demonstrating the unidirectional rotary motion of the two CBPQT4+/CBPQT2+• rings. In State I, [CBPQT-A]4+ and [CBPQT-B]4+ are positioned around the T and BPM units, respectively. Reduction of the V2+ units and the CBPQT4+ rings by injections (Step 1) of six electrons in total triggers both rings to undergo a clockwise rotation, leading to the formation (State II) of the reduced state [3]CMM7+6•. Subsequent oxidation by the removal (Step 2) of six electrons restores the Coulombic repulsion between the two rings and the loop, obliging [CBPQT-B]4+ to thread over (State III) the steric barrier (IPP) under thermal activation and eventually encircle T, while [CBPQT-A]4+ finds itself threaded around BPM, thus completing a 180˚ positional exchange between the two rings shown in State I′. A second redox cycle (Step 3 and 4) resets the system back to State I after the accomplishment of another 180˚ positional exchange between the two rings.

  • #1450. "Mechanically Bonded Materials (Stoddart)".

    • Summary of MSC collaborations with the Stoddart group up to ~2019.

D3-1. The Rotaxane-TTF Switch (Collaborations with Jim Heath and Fraser Stoddart)

Our first interactions involved the rotaxanes Stoddart designed to provide programmable molecular switches that were the basis of the colossally dense memory that Jim Heath and he developed in the early 2000’s.

D3-2. Barrier Hopping

We carried out a number of studies using both QM and MD using force field (FF) calculations to predict the barriers for station-hopping, monolayers of rotaxanes on Au(111) surfaces and refining the interpretations.

D3-3. Design of a three-station catenane, voltage tunable for three-color RGB molecular switch

Weiqiao Deng and I used our new understanding to design a three-station catenane (molecule with interlocking rings) where applying a sequence of redox potentials would cause a rotary motion of one ring with respect to the other. This was not published.

We also designed and published a three-station catenane that would turn red, green, or blue depending on the applied potential [#648]. We published this design and our predictions before Stoddart had done any experiments (but with him a co-author).

Later Stoddart synthesized this design and showed it to be a three-color molecule switchable by changing the applied potential, just as we had predicted. He published this experimental paper [#737] with the Deng and I as collaborators.

D3-4. Design of Mechanically Bonded Machines

As Stoddart was designing and synthesizing new mechanically bonded supramolecular moieties for use in designing nano machines, nano shuttles, inclusion complexes, artificial molecular muscles, nano receptor boxes, light-driven movements of nano assemblies, etc., our group was right with them in the trenches calculating various structures, interactions and dynamics, spectroscopic signatures and so on, to help us validate the structures and properties of our nano machine technology. (See: [#554, #623. #767, #783, #800, #819, #833, #968, #1031, #1040, #1061]) Stoddart told me that he considered that our efforts were indispensable to the progress of his experimental program.

D3-5. Establishing the 1:1 inclusion complex nature of CBPQT with TTF

A very important contribution of our theory was [#1160]. Another theory group had carried out low-level DFT calculations (not hybrid, no solvent) and claimed on this basis that the 1:1 inclusion complex of CBPQT [cyclobis(paraquat-p-phenylene)]with TTF (tetrathiafulvalene), on which Stoddart had published numerous experimental and theory papers, was incorrect. The other group claimed that the molecular components were complexed one with another but not an inclusion compound. Wei-Guang Liu jumped quickly onto this issue (along with Stoddart) and carried out a number of DFT calculations (hybrid and not) and under relevant solvent conditions. For each case, we calculated the complexation energetics and spectroscopic signatures. The resulting paper [#1160] established quite convincingly that the 1:1 inclusion complex is in fact the correct molecular structure. This removed a cloud of suspicion from Stoddart’s research program.

  • #1206. "Quantum Mechanical and Experimental Validation that Cyclobis(paraquat-p-phenylene) Forms a 1:1 Inclusion Complex with Tetrathiafulvalene".
D3-6. Design of Multi-radical using Mechanical Bonding

An area where our understanding of chemical bonding became quite important was Stoddart’s creation and characterization of stable molecular tetraradical and triradical systems. (See: [#834, #910, #991, #1010, #1062, #1093, #1161, #1181]) These unique structures are stabilized by mechanical bonding, leading to multiradical molecules in configurations where the radicals are prevented from bonding strongly. Such broken bond systems, however, are described very poorly with DFT theory alone. Here, we used our understanding of bonding to use DFT on the one structure for which DFT is valid (high spin, thus S=2 for the tetraradical). We then used this wavefunction to initialize ab initio CAS-SCF (same as GVB-CI) calculations to optimize the two singlet, three triplet, and quintet states for the tetraradical, leading to rigorous spin couplings. For these structures, we showed how the bonding was modified as the tetraradical was oxidized and reduced through nine distinct redox states. Further, we calculated the eight redox potentials – of which Stoddart was able to measure six in the laboratory – which proved to be in good agreement with his experimental results. This complex is the first stable tetraradical molecular species, demonstrating how the bonding between the four radicals depends on the redox state.
